Project Overview

Sunuka, Tanzania is a farming community that historically faced severe agricultural limitations during dry seasons, preventing farmers from increasing crop yields and threatening food security. Without reliable irrigation, crops depended entirely on seasonal rainfall — a gamble that left families vulnerable to drought and economic hardship.

Starting in 2018, EWB Cornell's International Team established a long-term partnership with the farming cooperative in Sunuka to design and implement a solar-powered irrigation system. Over multiple years of assessment trips, community engagement, and engineering design, the team delivered a system that transformed the community's agricultural capacity.

The Challenge

  • Dry season crop failure due to complete reliance on seasonal rainfall
  • No existing water infrastructure for agricultural use
  • Limited access to grid electricity for pump systems
  • Community needed a system maintainable with locally available skills and parts
  • Long-term sustainability required community ownership of the system

The Solution

The team designed and installed a solar-powered water pump system connected to irrigation piping that distributes water across the farming cooperative's fields. Solar panels eliminate dependence on the unreliable grid, while the system was designed with simplicity and local maintainability in mind.

The final phase included coordination with local contractors to complete tap stands along irrigation piping, giving individual farmers direct access points to the water supply within their plots.

🌾
Farming cooperative benefiting from year-round irrigation

Impact

  • Year-round crop production now possible regardless of rainfall
  • Significantly increased food security for the cooperative and surrounding community
  • Higher crop yields lead directly to greater farmer income and economic stability
  • System assessed as fully operational and community-maintained
  • Local maintenance knowledge transferred to cooperative members

Key Learnings

This project demonstrated the power of sustained community partnership over multiple years. By building relationships through repeated assessment trips beginning in 2018, the team gained the trust and detailed knowledge necessary to deliver an appropriate, lasting solution. The Tanzania project now serves as a model for how EWB Cornell approaches international development engineering.
