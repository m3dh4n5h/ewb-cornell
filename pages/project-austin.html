Project Overview

The Festival Beach Community Garden in East Austin serves as a vital community resource — providing fresh produce to local residents, reducing food insecurity, and creating green space in a densely urban neighborhood. However, the garden faces significant challenges from water insecurity and rising utility costs that threaten its long-term viability and the community it serves.

EWB Cornell's Domestic Team, in collaboration with civil and environmental engineering mentors and professional firms, is designing comprehensive water management infrastructure to make the garden climate-resilient and operationally sustainable.

The Challenge

  • Water insecurity threatens consistent crop production and garden operations
  • Rising potable water costs limit the garden's operational budget
  • Inadequate stormwater management causes flooding that damages beds
  • No rainwater harvesting or reclaimed water systems in place
  • Irrigation inefficiency wastes limited water resources

Our Engineering Solution

The team is developing a multi-faceted water management system that addresses both supply and quality concerns. The design integrates stormwater management, rainwater harvesting, reclaimed water reuse, and solar-powered pumping into a cohesive infrastructure plan that serves the full garden.

💧
Water management design using GIS and hydraulic modeling

Key Engineering Components

  • Stormwater management systems to reduce flooding and protect garden beds
  • Efficient drip irrigation networks for water conservation across all plots
  • Rainwater storage cisterns to capture and reuse precipitation
  • Reclaimed water infrastructure to reduce reliance on potable water
  • Solar-powered pumping solutions to minimize energy costs

Design Process

The team utilized CAD, GIS mapping, and hydraulic modeling tools to create detailed construction documents. Students collaborated closely with civil and environmental engineering mentors and professional engineering firms to ensure designs meet local codes and best practices. By Fall 2025, the team will have finalized full construction documents ready for implementation.

Community Impact

The completed infrastructure will reduce flooding, ensure dependable year-round irrigation, and dramatically decrease the garden's reliance on expensive potable water — making fresh, locally grown produce more accessible and affordable for the East Austin community long-term.
